II. Patterns, Expressions & Practice

1. NOUN이에요/예요. “am/are/is NOUN.”
Usage

NOUN이에요/예요” is used to identify someone or something.

Meaning

NOUN이에요/예요 means “(Someone) is NOUN” or “(Something) is NOUN.”
Here, “이에요” or “예요” is similar to “am; are; is” in English. Their base form (or dictionary form) is 이다 “to be.”

Form

Use the rule below to choose between 이에요 and 예요.

▶ Consonant-ending NOUN + 이에요
: 김민준이에요.
[ㄴ + 이에요]
▶ Vowel-ending NOUN + 예요
: 에이미예요.
[ㅣ + 예요]

Remember your 한글 lesson. Consonants are ㄱ, ㄴ, ㄷ, ㄹ, ㅁ, ㅂ… and vowels are ㅏ, ㅓ, ㅗ, ㅜ, ㅡ, ㅣ…
Korean English
(Subject) NOUN 이에요/예요. (Subject) am/are/is NOUN.
김민준이에요.
김민준이에요.
에이미예요.
한글이에요.

(It) is Hangeul.

Note 1
The subject often does not appear in a Korean sentence when it is understood among the speakers in the context.

Note 2
“NOUN이세요/ NOUN세요?” is the honorific form of NOUN이에요/ NOUN예요, respectively. You can use “NOUN이세요/ NOUN세요?” to show respect to the person who you are referring to. For more details, see Lesson 5. (Ⅱ.1)

You: 인턴이에요? Are you an intern?
You: 인턴이세요? Are you an intern? [Showing more respect]

2. NOUN이에요/예요. “NOUN.”
Usage

Use “NOUN이요/요” to answer the question in the simplest way, mentioning only the very item in question. Compare the following two answers using either ~이에요/~예요 or ~이요/~요.

Question: Who is that person?

    Answer 1: 김민준이에요.
    (He) is Kim Minjoon. [Sentence-form answer]
    Answer 2: 김민준이요.
    Kim Minjoon. [Simplest answer with NOUN]

Question: 성함이? Your name (is)?

    Answer 1: 에이미예요.
    (I) am Amy. [Sentence-form answer]
    Answer 2: 에이미요.
    Amy. [Simplest answer with NOUN]
Form

Use the rule below to choose between 이요 and 요.

▶ Consonant-ending NOUN + 이요
: 김민준이요.
[ㄴ + 이요]
▶ Vowel-ending NOUN + 요
: 에이미요.
[ㅣ + 요]

Note that Korean speakers of some dialects may use 요 in both cases.

Look at the following examples and see how ~이요/요 is used to respond.

  1. A: 누구예요?  Who is this?
    B: 지호요.  Jiho.

  2. A: 누구예요?  Who is this?
    B: 마이클이요.  Michael.

3. 성함이 어떻게 되세요? “May I have your name?
meaning

The literal meaning of “성함이 어떻게 되세요?” is “성함이 (name) 어떻게 (how) 되세요 (become)?” The meaning in the context is “May I have your name?

Usage

“성함이 어떻게 되세요?” is frequently used between adults (in a formal situation). Alternatively, “이름이 뭐예요?” is used for asking younger people’s names (in an informal situation). The literal meaning of “이름이 뭐예요?” is “이름이 (name) 뭐 (what) 예요 (is)?”

성함이 어떻게 되세요? May I have your name? (formal)

이름이 뭐예요? What is your name? (informal)

Look at the following examples and see how “성함이 어떻게 되세요?” and “이름이 뭐예요?” are used in the conversations.

  1. (At a conference)
    A: 성함이 어떻게 되세요?
    B: 김민준이에요.

  2. (At a K-pop club meeting on campus)
    A: 이름이 뭐예요?
    B: 미나예요.

4. 뭐예요? “What is it?”

You just learned “이름이 뭐예요?” (What is your name?). “뭐예요?” on its own means “What is (it)?”, which is a combination of “뭐” (what) and “예요” (is).

(Korean) 뭐예요?
Literal translation What is?
English translation What is (it)?

Look at the following examples and see how 뭐예요? is used.

  1. A: 뭐예요? (What is it?)
    B: 포토 앨범이에요. (It’s a photo album.)

  2. A: 뭐예요? (What is it?)
    B: 초콜릿 쿠키예요. (It’s a chocolate cookie.)

III. Culture

1. Greeting convention

Bowing is a common way of greeting in Korean, especially when one sees their superior or co-workers in the office. Shopkeepers regularly greet their customers with casual bowing to show respect. People also bow when they express thanks or offer apologies. Koreans bow at about 30 degrees when they greet, but the angle lowers to 90 degrees when the person wants to express more respect, give a sincere apology, or show gratitude. People around the same age do not generally bow to each other and just exchange greetings or shake hands.

2. Korean names

Koreans say their family name followed by their given name. They also address their full names when they introduce themselves.

Mark Kim

이은혜

For example,

민준: 안녕하세요? 김민준이에요.

How are you? I am Kim Minjoon.

Family name Given name

When it comes to Korean-Americans’ names such as “Mark Kim,” they often follow the American naming convention, 마크 김.

Given name Family name

Koreans don’t call names directly when they need to call older people during a conversation. Instead, they address the older people by their titles, such as “President Kim,” “Professor Lee,” or simply, “President” or “Professor.” This title calling practice is based on Confucian teachings. Confucius taught that one should respect their elders and that young people should learn from their elder’s wisdom. Even today, Koreans generally assume that the older the person is, the more experienced they are. Therefore, they address the older person with more gestures and expressions.

사장님 (company boss), 안녕하세요?
