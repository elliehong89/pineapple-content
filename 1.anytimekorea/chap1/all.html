1. NOUN이에요/예요. “am/are/is NOUN.”

Usage

“NOUN이에요/예요” is used to identify someone or something.

meaning

NOUN이에요/예요 means “(Someone) is NOUN” or “(Something) is NOUN.” 이에요 or 예요“ Here, ” is similar to “am; are; is” in English. Their base form (or dictionary form) is 이다 “to be.”

Form

Use the rule below to choose between 이에요 and 예요

▶ Consonant-ending NOUN + 이에요

김민준이에요.

[ㄴ + 이에요]

▶ Vowel-ending NOUN + 예요

:

에이미예요.

[ㅣ + 예요]

3. 성함이 어떻게 되세요? “May I have your name?”

meaning

The literal meaning of “성함이 어떻게 되세요?” is “성함이 (name) 어떻게 (how) 되세요 (become)?” The meaning in the context is “May I have your name?”

Usage

“성함이 어떻게 되세요?” is frequently used between adults (in a formal situation). Alternatively, “이름이 뭐예요?” is used for asking younger people’s names (in an informal situation). The literal meaning of “이름이 뭐예요?” is “이름이 (name) 뭐 (what) 예요 (is)?”

성함이 어떻게 되세요?
May I have your name? (formal)
이름이 뭐예요?
What is your name? (informal)

Look at the following examples and see how “성함이 어떻게 되세요?” and “이름이 뭐예요?” are used in the conversations.

1. (At a conference)

A: 성함이 어떻게 되세요?

B: 김민준이에요.

2. (At a K-pop club meeting on campus)

A: 이름이 뭐예요?

B: 미나예요.

4. 뭐예요? “What is it?”

You just learned “이름이 뭐예요?” (What is your name?). “뭐예요?” on its own means “What is (it)?”, which is a combination of “뭐” (what) and “예요” (is).

(Korean)

(Referring to something) 뭐예요?

(Literal translation)

(Referring to something) What is?

III. Culture

1. Greeting convention

Bowing is a common way of greeting in Korean, especially when one sees their superior or co-workers in the office. Shopkeepers regularly greet their customers with casual bowing to show respect. People also bow when they express thanks or offer apologies. Koreans bow at about 30 degrees when they greet, but the angle lowers to 90 degrees when the person wants to express more respect, give a sincere apology, or show gratitude. People around the same age do not generally bow to each other and just exchange greetings or shake hands.

Kim Family name

Minjoon. Given name

Koreans don’t call names directly when they need to call older people during a conversation. Instead, they address the older people by their titles, such as “President Kim,” “Professor Lee,” or simply, “President” or “Professor.” This title calling practice is based on Confucian teachings. Confucius taught that one should respect their elders and that young people should learn from their elder’s wisdom. Even today, Koreans generally assume that the older the person is more experienced they are. Therfore, they address the older person with more gestures and expressions.

사장님(company boss), 안녕하세요?
