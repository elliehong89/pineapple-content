II. Patterns, Expressions & Practice

1. place name + -에 “in/on/at/to + place name”

When the location marker -에 is used after a place name, it means “at,” “on,” “in,” or “to” depending on the context. Here are some commonly used expressions: -에 살아요, -에 있어요, and -에 가요.

1. -에 살아요

to live in ~

저는 뉴욕에 살아요.

I live in New York.

마이클은 서울에 살아요.

Michael lives in Seoul.

어디(에) 살아요?

Where do you live? [Lit., At where do (you) live?]

어디(에) 살세요?

Where do you live? [to your senior]

Note1

어디 without -에 is frequently used in colloquial conversation.

2. -에 있어요

to be in ~ [Lit., exist in ~]

저는 지금 하와이에 있어요.

I am in Hawaii now.

마크는 서울에 있어요.

Mark is in Seoul.

지금 어디(에) 있어요?

Where are you now? [Lit., At where do (you) exist?]

3. -에 가요

to go to

저는 보스턴에 가요.

I’m going to Boston.

지금 수업에 가요.

I’m going to class now.

지금 미팅(에) 가요.

I’m going to meeting now.

파티(에) 가요.

I’m going to a party.

어디(에) 가요?

Where are you going? [Lit., To where do (you) go?]

2. NOUN은/는/이/가 ... 있어요 & 없어요

있어요 (dictionary form: 있다) carries two different meanings according to the context: "to be; exist; live" and "to have; possess."

-아요/~어요 form

(to be; exist; live)

The opposite meaning of 있어요 (dictionary form: 있다) is 없어요 (dictionary form: 없다).

-아요/~어요 form
없다
(to not be; not exist; not live)

없어요
(to not have; not possess)

1. In the following examples 있다 or 없다 has the meaning of existence (“to be/ exist/ live”) or (“to not be/ not exist/ not live”) Note that the marker -은/는/이/가 often drops in casual talk.

(1)
제니는 사무실에 있어요.
Jenny is in the office.
제니는 사무실에 없어요.
Jenny is not in the office.
(2)
A:
제니 여기 있어요?
Is Jenny here?
B:
여기 없어요.
(She) is not here?
(3)
A:
제니 어디 있어요?
Where is Jenny?
B:
사무실에 있어요.
(She) is in the office.

Note

The verb "to be" in English has two meanings: (1) existence (e.g. God is.) and (2) identification (e.g. He is a student.). However, unlike English, two different words are used in Korean: (1) 있어요 to mean existence and (2) ~이에요/~예요 to mean identification.

에이미는 뉴욕에 있어요.
Amy is in New York.
[Existence]
에이미는 학생이에요.
Amy is in a student.
[Identification: 에이미 = 학생]

The opposite meanings of the above expressions are:

에이미는 뉴욕에 없어요.
Amy is not in New York.
[Non-existence]
에이미는 학생이 아니에요.
Amy is not a student.
[Identification: 에이미 ≠ 학생]

3. 근데 “but”

근데 is the shortened form of 그런데. It means “but” and is used at the beginning of the sentence. In colloquial and daily conversations, 근데 may be more frequently used than 그런데. However, 그런데 is more commonly used in writing and formal speech.

1.
침대 있어요. 근데 냉장고는 없어요.
I have a bed. But as for the refrigerator, (I) don’t have (it).
2.
커피 없어요. 근데 주스는 있어요.
I don’t have coffee. But as for the juice, I have (it).
3.
기숙사 좋아요. 그런데 좀 비싸요.
My dormitory is good But it’s a little expensive.
4.
숙제가 좀 많아요. 근데 선생님은 좋아요.
The homework is a little bit sizable. But as for the teacher, (he/she) is good.

Your friend came over to your house. He/she asks for something. Tell him/her Exercise that you don't have it but you have an alternative.

4. NOUN은/는/이/가 + 어때요? "How is ~?"

You can use "~어때요" " to ask how someone is (doing) or how the conditions of things are. Note that the marker -은/는/이/가 often drops in casual talk.

한국어 클래스(는) 어때요?

Korean language class how is?

How is (your) Korean class?

김 선생님(은) 어때요?

Teacher Kim how is?

How is Mr. Kim?

아파트(가) 어때요?

Apartment how is?

How is (your) apartment?

서울 날씨(는) 어때요?

Seoul weather how is?

How is the weather in Seoul?

비빔밥(이) 어때요?

Bibimbap how is?

How is bibimbap?

You can also use ~어때요? when you make a suggestion such as "How about ~?"

내일(은) 어때요?

tomorrow how is?

How about tomorrow?

비빔밥(은) 어때요?

Bibimbap how is?

How about (eating/ordering) bibimbap?

STEP 1.

Talk to three classmates one at a time, and ask where they live, the size of their room, and the household items available. Please act out and create a meaningful conversation just as you practiced in the Guided Conversation.

Try to make your conversation flow naturally, following the given guidelines.

1. Ask one’s residence type (e.g, 기숙사; 아파트; 주택 house)
