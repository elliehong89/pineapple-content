1. ~잖아요. “you know, ~”

Usage

~잖아요 is used when you want to confirm something that you believe the listener is already aware of. Depending on the context, ~잖아요 can be translated as “you know~,” “Didn’t you know~?” or “Did you forget~?”

2. 여기; 거기; 저기 here; there; over there

meaning

여기, 거기, and 저기 are place words meaning “here”, “there”, and “over there”, respectively.

[1] 여기 here

Use 여기 to point out person or thing that is closer to you.

남자:

리모콘 어디 있어요?

Where is the remote control?

여자:

여기 있어요.

(It) is here.

[2] 거기 there

Use 거기 to point out person or thing that is closer to the listener.

남자:

리모콘 어디 있어요?

Where is the remote control?

여자:

거기 있어요.

(It) is there (near you).

Note1

거기 can also be used when referring to a place that was already mentioned in the conversation. See the examples below.

(Talking about a Korean restaurant “Seoul Garden.”)

A: “서울 가든” 알아요?

Do you know Seoul Garden?

B: 네. 알아요.

Yes, I know.

A: 거기 어때요?

How is that place?

B: 거기 괜찮아요.

That place is alright.

(Talking about a Korean restaurant “Seoul Garden.”)

A: 에이미 씨, 집이 어디예요?

Amy, where is your hometown?

B: 시카고예요. 부모님이 거기 계세요.

It’s Chicago. My parents are there

[3] 저기 over there

Use 저기 to point out person or thing that is away from both the speaker and the listener.

남자:

리모콘 어디 있어요?

Where is the remote control?

여자:

저기 있어요.

(It) is over there.

4. 혹시 ~ “I wonder ~; Do you happen to ~?”

혹시 is an expression that literally means “by any chance.” By saying 혹시 at the beginning of a sentence, you can make your question or request more subtle and less direct. By adding 혹시, you sound more polite during conversation

혹시 마크 씨 전화번호 아세요?

Do you happen to know Mark’s phone number?

혹시 스테이플러 있으세요?

Do you happen to have a stapler?

혹시 오늘 시간 있으세요?

Do you by any chance have time today?

혹시 영어 하세요?

Do you by any chance speak English?

5. 좀 ~으세요/~세요. “Please do ~.”

You can sound more polite by adding 좀 (literally meaning “a bit; somewhat”) in your request. A request made with 좀 gives more polite and less blunt impression to the listener. Place 좀 immediately before the verb.

1.
좀 드세요.
Please eat (this).
도넛 좀 드세요.
Please eat some donuts
2.
좀 보여 주세요.
Please show (that) (to me).
사진 좀 보여 주세요.
Please show the photo to me.
3.
좀 주세요.
Please give (that) (to me).
물 좀 주세요.
Please give me some water.

6. 잠시만요. “Just a second, please.”

잠시만요 “Just a second, please.” is used when you ask the listener to wait for a moment. 잠시 literally means “a short time” and 만 means “only.” For the same purpose, you can also say 잠시 기다리세요 “Please wait a moment.” or 잠시만 기다리세요 “Please wait just a moment,” using the verb 기다리다 “to wait.”

1.
A:
신분증 있으세요?
Do you have your identification card? [신분증 ID card]
B:
네. 잠시만요.
Yes. Just a second.
2.
A:
물 좀 주세요.
Please give me some water.
B:
네. 잠시만 기다리세요.
Sure. Please wait just a moment.
3.
A:
샘플 좀 보여주세요.
Please show me a sample.
B:
잠시만요.
Just one moment, please.

Note1

You can also say 잠깐 or 잠깐만 instead of 잠시 or 잠시만, respectively.

It is common for Koreans to call each other using kinship terms even if they are not a “real” relative. For example, 이모 or 고모 is frequently used to call an owner of a small family-run soju bar where they sell drinks and dinner items. Some people call older an lady 어머니 and older gentleman 아버지 to be polite and friendly. When you hear someone referring to an old lady 어머니 on the street, it may be the case that the old lady is not the actual mother of the person!
