1. What is Hangeul?

Hangeul (or the Korean alphabet) refers to the Korean writing and sound system. Hangeul was created in 1443 by King Sejong (1418-1450), the fourth King of the Joseon Dynasty of Korea. Before the 15th century, Koreans did not have their own writing system to represent their spoken language and, therefore, borrowed Chinese characters to record their speech. However, as the Korean language is structurally and phonetically widely different from Chinese, it was difficult and inconvenient for Koreans to learn Chinese characters. It was also difficult to represent Korean speech and ideas with Chinese characters. For this reason, King Sejong invented the Korean alphabet, consisting of consonants and vowels, which were called Hunminjeongeum, meaning “the correct sounds to teach the people.” Hangeul is the new name for Hunminjeongeum. Hangeul is widely cited as an excellent writing and sound system that is logical, concise, and easy to learn. Each alphabet in Hangeul represents one sound; once you learn the sound value of each consonant and vowel, you can easily read and write Korean letters. King Sejong made a statement in his 1446 document: “A wise man can acquaint himself with Hangeul letters before the morning is over; even an ignorant man can learn them in the span of ten days.” Due to the simplicity and efficiency of Hangeul, both South and North Koreas maintain exceptionally high literacy rates. Koreans commemorate and celebrate the invention of Hangeul every year on October 9th. In 1997, the United Nations Educational, Scientific, and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) designated Hunminjeongeum Haerye (a book providing explanations and examples of Hunminjeongeum) as an international archive property.
