1. What is Hangeul?

Hangeul (or the Korean alphabet) refers to the Korean writing and sound system. Hangeul was created in 1443 by King Sejong (1418-1450), the fourth King of the Joseon Dynasty of Korea. Before the 15th century, Koreans did not have their own writing system to represent their spoken language and, therefore, borrowed Chinese characters to record their speech. However, as the Korean language is structurally and phonetically widely different from Chinese, it was difficult and inconvenient for Koreans to learn Chinese characters. It was also difficult to represent Korean speech and ideas with Chinese characters. For this reason, King Sejong invented the Korean alphabet, consisting of consonants and vowels, which were called Hunminjeongeum, meaning “the correct sounds to teach the people.” Hangeul is the new name for Hunminjeongeum. Hangeul is widely cited as an excellent writing and sound system that is logical, concise, and easy to learn. Each alphabet in Hangeul represents one sound; once you learn the sound value of each consonant and vowel, you can easily read and write Korean letters. King Sejong made a statement in his 1446 document: “A wise man can acquaint himself with Hangeul letters before the morning is over; even an ignorant man can learn them in the span of ten days.” Due to the simplicity and efficiency of Hangeul, both South and North Koreas maintain exceptionally high literacy rates. Koreans commemorate and celebrate the invention of Hangeul every year on October 9th. In 1997, the United Nations Educational, Scientific, and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) designated Hunminjeongeum Haerye (a book providing explanations and examples of Hunminjeongeum) as an international archive property.

Pronouncing two consonants on the bottom in a syllable that are followed by a vowel

In two consonants, both consonants are pronounced. The first consonant is not released, whereas the second consonant is carried over to the next syllable and pronounced together with the vowels.

5.4 Markers in Korean sentences

There are a number of grammatical markers in Korean. For example, “-이/-가” is attached after a noun to mark something or someone as the subject in a sentence. It is called “the subject marker(See Lesson 7).” “-을/-를” is attached after a noun to mark something or someone as the object in a sentence. It is called “the object marker” (See Lesson 12). “-에” or “-에서” is attached after a place name to mark a location in a sentence. It is called “the location marker” (See Lesson 6), and the list goes on.

(1) Subject and Object Markers

There is no English equivalent to the subject marker (-이/-가) or the object marker (-을/-를). In English, the subject or the object of a sentence is determined based on the placement of the noun in a sentence. The subject is someone/something (as a “doer”) that comes before the verb. The object is someone/something (as a “recipient”) that comes after the verb. However, in Korean, someone/something marked with -이/-가 is the subject and someone/something marked with -을/-를 is the object regardless of the placement. Notice the difference between the two languages in the following examples.

5.6. Questions in Korean

In English, the “Subject-Verb” word order changes to “Verb-Subject” in a question sentence. However, word order does not change in Korean. That is, both a statement and a question use the same word order and sentence ending. Instead, falling intonation is used for a statement and rising intonation is used for a question.

Brian Subject

is Verb

a student.

is Verb

Statement :

먹어요. Verb

Amy is eating.
Question :

에이미가 Subject

먹어요? Verb

(rising intonation on 먹어요)

Amy is eating.
