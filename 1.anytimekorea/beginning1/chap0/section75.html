5.2 Verbs and adjectives in Korean

(1) Dictionary form and verb/adjective base

When Korean verbs and adjectives end in "-다," they are in "dictionary form." From the dictionary form, we separate the "base" by dropping "-다."

Dictionary form Base 다 ending Meaning
가다 (Verb) 가 다 to go
먹다 (Verb) 먹 다 to eat
바쁘다 (Verb) 바쁘 다 to be busy
괜찮다 (Verb) 괜찮 다 to be alright

Because both verbs and adjectives end in -다, you can distinguish them based on their meaning. For example, verbs are "actions" or "processes," whereas adjectives are "states" (i.e., size, weight, quality, quantity, shape, appearance, perception, and emotion). This will be covered in Ⅱ. 1 in Lesson 4.

(2) Sentence ending

When making sentences in Korean. you must attach a sentence ending (that indicates the end of a sentence) to a verb and an adjective. The most common sentence ending ~아요/~어요 form is shown below. The various forms of the sentence endings are shown in "5.3 Different levels of speech and sentence endings in Korean" on the next page.

에이미는 한국 음식을 먹어요.

Amy Korean food eat

Amy eats Korean food.

에이미는 괜찮아요.

Amy be okay

Amy is okay.

The following table shows how you can conjugate a verb and an adjective for "~아요/~어요 sentence ending."

Base + ~아요/~어요 ending Meaning
먹다 (Verb) 먹어요 to eat
괜찮다 (Adjective)) 괜찮아요 to be okay

In Lessons 1-3, all of the verbs and adjectives are listed with the form of ~아요/~어요 ending in Ⅰ. New Words & Expressions. In Lesson 4, you will study Korean verbs and adjectives and their ~아요/~어요 forms in detail.

(2) Non-honorific (formal/informal) styles

You may use “a non-honorific style” to a person who has a lower social position or is younger than you. When ~는다 and ~아 styles are used in casual conversations, there is no difference in formality. However, “the ~는다 style” can be used in writing, formal announcements, or military commands, while ~아 style is mostly used in casual conversations.
