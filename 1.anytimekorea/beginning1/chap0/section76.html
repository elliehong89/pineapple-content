5.4 Markers in Korean sentences

There are a number of grammatical markers in Korean. For example, “-이/-가” is attached after a noun to mark something or someone as the subject in a sentence. It is called “the subject marker(See Lesson 7).” “-을/-를” is attached after a noun to mark something or someone as the object in a sentence. It is called “the object marker” (See Lesson 12). “-에” or “-에서” is attached after a place name to mark a location in a sentence. It is called “the location marker” (See Lesson 6), and the list goes on.

(1) Subject and Object Markers

There is no English equivalent to the subject marker (-이/-가) or the object marker (-을/-를). In English, the subject or the object of a sentence is determined based on the placement of the noun in a sentence. The subject is someone/something (as a “doer”) that comes before the verb. The object is someone/something (as a “recipient”) that comes after the verb. However, in Korean, someone/something marked with -이/-가 is the subject and someone/something marked with -을/-를 is the object regardless of the placement. Notice the difference between the two languages in the following examples.

English:

Brian Subject

reads Verb

a book Object

in the library. preposition+a place

Korean:

a.

브라이언이 Brian Subject

도서관에서 the library-in

책을 a book Object

읽어요. reads Verb

b.

도서관에서 the library-in

책을 a book Object

브라이언이 Brian Subject

읽어요. reads Verb

(2) Location Markers

Location markers function similarly to English prepositions (e.g. in, at, on). However, the Korean markers are attached after something or someone in a sentence, whereas English prepositions appear before something or someone. 5.5. Helping (or Auxiliary) verbs

Another distinctive feature of Korean is that helping (or auxiliary) verbs are attached after the main verbs/adjectives. In example 2 below, the future tense of the verb 먹다 “to eat” is expressed with the helping verb 을 거예요. (indicated as helping V. below) and it is attached after the main verb.

5.6. Questions in Korean

In English, the “Subject-Verb” word order changes to “Verb-Subject” in a question sentence. However, word order does not change in Korean. That is, both a statement and a question use the same word order and sentence ending. Instead, falling intonation is used for a statement and rising intonation is used for a question.

(1) English
Statement :

Brian Subject

is Verb

a student.

Question :

is Verb

Brian Subject

a student?

(2) Korean
Statement :

에이미가 Subject

먹어요. Verb

(falling intonation on 먹어요)

Amy is eating.
Question :

에이미가 Subject

먹어요? Verb

(rising intonation on 먹어요)

Amy is eating.

5.7. Subject omission

The subject in a Korean sentence is often dropped when it is contextually obvious or understood between the speakers. In the following conversation, Speaker A drops the subject “브라이언” in the second sentence because “브라이언” was already mentioned in his first sentence.

A:

Subject 브라이언은 Brian

학생이에요. student-is

Subject (브라이언은)

보스턴대학교 Boston University

학생이에요. student-is

Brian is a student. (He) is a Boston University student.
B:

아~ 네. Oh ~ I see.
