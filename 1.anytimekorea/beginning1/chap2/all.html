II. Patterns, Expressions & Practice

1. A은/는 B이에요/예요. “A is B.”

Usage

This is an extended explanation of “Ⅱ.1.NOUN 이에요/예요.” in Lesson 1. When you introduce or identify someone or something, you can say [Someone or Something] A 은/는 [one’s name or its name] B 이에요/예요. Thus, when you introduce or identify yourself, you can say 저는 A [your name] B 이에요/예요.

저는 제시카예요.

I as for Jessica am

I am Jessica. [Lit., As for me, (I) am Jessica.]

You can drop 저는 when it is understood by the listener in the context.

제시카예요.

(I) am Jessica

meaning

A은/는 B이에요/예요 means “A is B” or “A equals B.” 이에요/예요 is attached to the preceding word with no space.

Form

Use the rule below to choose between 은 and 는.

▶ Consonant-ending NOUN + 이에요

:

김민준이에요.

[ㄴ + 이에요]

Kim Minjoon is Korean.

▶ Vowel-ending NOUN + 예요

:

에이미예요.

지하철 subway로 가요.

Kim Minjoon is Korean.

2. Name + 씨 “Mr. ... ; Mrs. ... ; Miss ...”

Usage

By adding 씨 at the end of someone’s name, you can show respect to the person. Note that 씨 should be used to a person of the same age or a person who is younger than you.

First name + 씨: 샤오밍씨 (O)

First name + 씨: 황샤오밍씨 (O) appropriate

Note 1

If [name + 씨] form is used to a person who is older than you, you may sound rude. Instead, refer to them as [their job title + 님].

Common titles of people

president = 사장님

director = 감독님

professor = 교수님

teacher = 선생님

parents = 부모님

Note 2

Attach 씨] after someone’s first name or full name but not after someone’s last name. 황 님] (X) inappropriate

In addition, you shouldn’t use 씨 after your own name because it is inappropriate.

3. ~은요?/~는요? “What about ~?; How about ~?”

meaning

~은요? literally means “as for ~?” but is best translated as “What about ~?” or “How about ~?”

샤오밍 씨는요?

Mr. Xiaoming as for

What about (you), Xiaoming?

Form

Use the rule below to choose between 은요? and 는요?

▶ Consonant-ending word + 은요?
마이클은요?
[ㄹ + 은요]
What about (you), Michael?
▶ Vowel-ending word + 는요?
마이클 씨는요?
[ㄹ + 는요]
What about (you), Michael?

Note 2

In Korean, the pronoun “you” is not frequently used. In place of “you,” use one’s name, job title (e.g., teacher 선생님, manager 매니저, section chief 부장님, etc), or family term (e.g., older brother 형/오빠, older sister 누나/언니, aunt 이모/고모, uncle 삼촌, etc.).

린다 씨는요?

What about (you), Linda?

선생님은요?

What about (you), Linda?

형은요?

What about (you), older brother?

4. Questions in Korean

In English, the subject-verb(S-V) word order switches when you change a statement sentence (S-V order) into a question (V-S order). However, in Korean the S-V order remains the same in both a statement and a question. To change a statement into a question, all you have to do is simply raise the intonation at the end of the sentence. (You also studied this in 5.6 Questions in Korean in Reading and writing Hangeul.)

If the question is a yes-no question, you can respond with either 네 (or 예) for "yes" or 아뇨 (or 아니요) for "no."

III. Culture

Diversity in Korea

Korea had been considered relatively homogeneous in its ethnicity and culture for a long time. However, Korea has experienced a greater and steady influx of foreign workers and immigrants since the 1990s, and the country is now becoming more and more ethnically diverse

When talking about someone’s ethnicity or nationality, you can use 사람 (native Korean word meaning person) or 인 (Sino-Korean word meaning person) after the name of the country. Notice that [nationality + 사람] has a space in between whereas [nationality + 인 ] does not.
