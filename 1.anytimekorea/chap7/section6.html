II. Patterns, Expressions & Practice

1. The subject-focus marker : -이/-가

Usage

Use -이-가to mark someone or something as the subject of a sentence.

1.

브라이언 Subject

이 학생이에요.

Brian Subject

is a student..
2.

에이미 Subject

가 바빠요.

Amy Subject

is busy.

When the subject is marked with -이/-가, the subject is focused as “new information” in the sentence.

1.
A:
누가 학생이에요?
Who is a student?
B:

브라이언 Subject

이 학생이에요.
Brian is a student.
[Interpretation: It is Brian who is a student.]
2.
A:
누가 바빠요?
Who is busy?
B:

에이미 Subject

가 바빠요.
Amy is busy.
[Interpretation: It is Amy who is busy.]
Form

Use the rule below to choose between -이 and 가

▶ Consonant-ending word + -이
: 마이클이
[ㄹ + 이]
서울에 살아요.
Michael lives in Seoul.
▶ Vowel-ending word + -가
: 민수가
[ㅜ + 가]
뉴욕에 살아요.
Minsoo lives in New York.

Note1

There are some irregular variations when the subject is combined with the subject marker, -가. When the subject marker -가 is added to -저 (I) or 나 (I), they become 제 가 and 내가.

▶ 저 ‘I’ (humble form) + -가 → 제가

▶ 나 ‘I’ (plain form) + -가 → 내가

(Talking to your senior)

제가 바빠요.

I am busy.

(Talking to your younger brother)

내가 바빠.

I am busy.

Note2

-이. -가 is often dropped in colloquial and daily conversations. But it is often used in writing and formal speech.

(Writing/Formal speech)

에이미가 바빠요.

Amy is busy.

(Colloquial conversation)

에이미  바빠요.

(Writing/Formal speech)

브라이언이 학생이에요.

Brian is a student.

(Colloquial conversation)

브라이언  학생이에요.

(Writing/Formal speech)

이름이 뭐예요?

What is (your) name?

(Colloquial conversation)

이름  뭐예요?

(Writing/Formal speech)

제니가 가요.

Jenny goes

(Colloquial conversation)

제니  가요.
